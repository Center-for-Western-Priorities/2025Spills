Trend

Spill counts have risen sharply since 2021

The number of historical spill records filed each year was relatively flat from 2016 through 2020, then accelerated dramatically. From 2020 to 2025 filings increased more than fifteenfold, from 125 to 1,948 unique spills per year. This reflects a wave of decommissioning activity across Colorado's aging DJ Basin well fleet, as older wells reach end of life and operators are required to plug, cap, and remediate sites before closure.

Geography

Weld County accounts for 87% of all historical spills

The geographic distribution is highly concentrated. Weld County, the heart of Colorado's Denver-Julesburg Basin, has 5,303 of the 6,084 total historical spill records. The next largest county, Adams, has just 187. This reflects both the density of oil and gas development in the DJ Basin and the scale of ongoing abandonment and reclamation activity there.

Operators

Three operators account for 76% of historical spill records

Noble Energy (2,301), Kerr McGee Oil & Gas (1,672), and PDC Energy (665) together account for nearly three-quarters of all historical spill records on file. All three are major DJ Basin operators with large legacy well inventories. Noble Energy was acquired by Chevron in 2020, and Kerr McGee's assets were absorbed into Crestone Peak Resources and then Civitas Resources, meaning many of these historical liabilities now sit with successor companies.

Infrastructure & Land

Tank batteries and wells are the most common failure points

Tank batteries (33%) and wells (30%) together account for nearly two-thirds of historical spill sites, with flowline systems contributing another 19%. Almost all historical spills, 96%, occurred on private land, with federal and state lands representing a small fraction. This has implications for landowner notification and long-term remediation obligations.

Seasonality

Spring and fall see the most spill discoveries

April is the peak month for historical spill discoveries, likely reflecting both spring construction season starting up and spring thaw exposing contamination frozen or buried under snow. October is a secondary peak, coinciding with fall site work before winter shutdowns. February sees the fewest discoveries, likely reflecting winter field slowdowns.

Key finding

Why are historical spills being discovered now?

Analysis of the spill description field across all 6,767 records reveals a striking pattern: nearly every historical spill was found not when it happened, but years or decades later, when someone dug up the ground. Active leak detection during operations accounts for less than 1% of discoveries. The vast majority are uncovered by construction, decommissioning, or regulatory cleanup work.

The core finding: Colorado's historical spill record is largely a paper trail of contamination that sat silently underground, sometimes for decades, until decommissioning work forced it into the open. These are not spills being caught and reported in real time. They are legacy contamination events that regulatory site closure requirements are finally surfacing.
  • 56%
    Deconstruction / tank removal Workers deconstructing a tank battery find contaminated soil beneath produced water vaults, dump lines, or tanks. The language is nearly formulaic across thousands of filings: "while deconstructing the [location] tank battery, soil with historical petroleum hydrocarbon impacts were encountered beneath the [vessel/vault/dumpline]." The contamination is almost always described as pre-existing with no active leak at the time of discovery.
  • ~20%
    Plugging, abandonment, and cut-and-cap operations A well is being formally plugged and shut in, or subject to a COGCC-mandated "cut and cap" wellhead closure procedure, and impacted soil is found beneath the wellhead, separator, or vault. The contamination is typically described as historical with unknown volume, and remediation is scheduled as a follow-on activity.
  • 4%
    Construction and excavation activities Routine construction work, including new pipelines, surface upgrades, and road grading, unexpectedly turns up hydrocarbon-impacted soil. These are typically incidental discoveries rather than targeted investigations.
  • 1%
    Pressure testing and integrity testing Flowline pressure tests or vault integrity tests reveal a failed component, which upon excavation exposes a historical release in the surrounding soil or groundwater.
  • 1%
    Site reclamation, pit closure, and emissions upgrades Closing an old production pit, conducting a Form 27 remediation project, or performing an emissions control upgrade triggers excavation that exposes legacy contamination not previously on record.
  • <1%
    COGCC field inspection A state field inspector spots pooled fluids or stained soil during a routine site visit and initiates a report. These represent a very small share of discoveries, suggesting inspection capacity is not the primary driver of detection.

Explaining the trend

Why the sharp rise in filings from 2021 onward?

The more-than-fifteenfold increase in historical spill filings between 2020 and 2025 is not primarily a sign that more spills are happening. It reflects several converging forces that are accelerating how quickly legacy contamination gets documented.

  • 1
    An aging well fleet entering mass decommissioning The DJ Basin saw a major drilling boom in the 2010s. Many of those wells, and the older legacy wells predating the boom, are now reaching end of productive life. When an operator plugs and abandons a well or deconstructs a tank battery, COGCC rules require environmental assessment of the site. As the volume of plugging and abandonment activity has climbed sharply since 2021, so has the volume of historical releases being uncovered and documented.
  • 2
    Stricter COGCC rules after SB 181 (2019) Colorado's landmark 2019 oil and gas reform law directed the COGCC to prioritize public health and environmental protection and led to a comprehensive rewrite of state rules. Enhanced site assessment requirements, stronger Form 27 closure protocols, and tighter remediation standards all mean that activities which might previously have closed out with minimal investigation now trigger more thorough soil and groundwater sampling, and more historical releases being identified and reported.
  • 3
    Cut-and-cap program scaling up COGCC's "cut and cap" rule requires operators to physically cut and cap wellheads at decommissioned sites. As this program has ramped up across Weld County's large inventory of older wells, each site assessment creates another opportunity to find and report soil contamination that has been present for years.
  • 4
    Operator consolidation surfacing inherited liabilities Several large acquisitions in the DJ Basin, notably Chevron's purchase of Noble Energy in 2020 and Civitas Resources' consolidation of Kerr McGee and Crestone legacy assets, brought new scrutiny to inherited well portfolios. Post-acquisition due diligence and accelerated decommissioning programs have pushed large backlogs of legacy contamination into the COGCC reporting system.
What the trend tells us: The rise in historical spill filings is largely good news from a regulatory standpoint. It means the decommissioning and remediation pipeline is working. But it also underscores how much historic contamination was never caught in real time, and how much legacy liability remains embedded in Colorado's oil and gas landscape, most of it on private land, and most of it concentrated in Weld County.
