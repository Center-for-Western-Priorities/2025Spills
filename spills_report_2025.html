State Report

Colorado

Colorado reported 338 oil and gas spills in 2025, continuing a trend of elevated spill totals since 2016. Weld County, home to the Denver-Julesburg Basin, accounted for half of all reported incidents. Two operators with recent acquisitions, QB Energy and TEP Rocky Mountain, drove dramatic year-over-year increases in both spill counts and volumes.

Trend

Colorado Spills Per Year, 2014–2025

Colorado's annual spill count spiked in 2017 and has remained elevated. After a dip during the COVID-19 slowdown in 2020, reports climbed back above 400 before declining slightly in 2024 and 2025. The state has recorded nearly 3,900 spills since 2014.

Source: Colorado Oil and Gas Conservation Commission (COGCC), 2025. Spill Type: Recent.
Volume

Total Volume Spilled Per Year (Gallons), 2021–2025

Volume data is a minimum estimate based on disclosed amounts. 2022 saw the highest reported volume at 1.46 million gallons. After a sharp decline in 2024, volumes rebounded in 2025, driven largely by a single large spill associated with QB Energy.

Note: Volume figures reflect the minimum amount reported to COGCC. Many spills list an unknown volume. They are most useful for comparing relative impact across operators and years, not as a precise environmental measure.
Source: COGCC, 2025.
Operators

Spills by Operator, 2025

The top five operators accounted for 45% of all 2025 spills. QB Energy, formed in 2024 to acquire Caerus Oil and Gas assets, led all operators with 46 spills. Kerr McGee and PDC Energy, both long-established legacy operators, reported the second and third most spills.

Geography

Spills by County, 2025

Weld County recorded 170 spills, accounting for 50% of Colorado's total. Garfield County in the Piceance Basin was second with 61. The remaining spills were spread across 19 additional counties.

Counties with at least one reported spill. Source: COGCC, 2025.
Land Ownership

Spills by Surface Owner, 2025

81% of spills occurred on private land, reflecting the concentration of DJ Basin drilling in areas with private surface ownership. 10% occurred on federal land managed by the Bureau of Land Management or US Forest Service.

Source: COGCC, 2025.
Spill Size

Spill Size Distribution, 2025

Most oil spills were small, under five barrels. The majority of produced water spills also fell in the one-to-100-barrel range. A meaningful share of both substances list an unknown volume, reflecting gaps in self-reported disclosure.

Oil spills by size (barrels). Source: COGCC, 2025.

Year-Over-Year

Volume Spilled by Top Operators: 2024 vs. 2025 (Gallons)

TEP Rocky Mountain and Laramie Energy both saw large volume increases in 2025. Scout Energy and Caerus Piceance saw substantial decreases. QB Energy, absent from 2024 data due to its formation that year, reported 445,000 gallons in 2025.

Operators with significant activity in 2024 or 2025. Source: COGCC, 2025.
Year-Over-Year

Biggest Volume Increases, 2024–2025 (Gallons)

TEP Rocky Mountain LLC, which acquired Terra Energy Partners' Piceance Basin assets, led all operators in volume increase. Laramie Energy and NGL Water Solutions DJ also saw notable jumps.

Source: COGCC, 2025.
Year-Over-Year

Biggest Volume Decreases, 2024–2025 (Gallons)

Scout Energy Management LLC reduced its reported volume by more than 98,000 gallons. Caerus Piceance LLC, the predecessor company to QB Energy in the Piceance Basin, reported a near-total drop as operations transitioned.

Source: COGCC, 2025.
State Report

Wyoming

Wyoming reported 786 oil and gas spills in 2025, a slight decrease from 825 in 2024. Converse and Campbell counties, home to the Powder River Basin, led all counties. Merit Energy Company was the top spiller by volume with more than 300,000 gallons of oil and produced water discharged.

Geography

Spills by County: 2024 vs. 2025

Converse County leads Wyoming in reported spill incidents, followed closely by Campbell County. Both counties sit atop the Powder River Basin, Wyoming's most active oil-producing region. Laramie County fell sharply from 69 to 33 spills. Carbon County increased from 73 to 83.

Top counties by 2025 spill count. Source: Wyoming Oil and Gas Conservation Commission (WOGCC), 2025.
Operators

Top Operators by Number of Spills, 2025

Crowheart led Wyoming operators with 102 spills, down from 145 in 2024. Continental reported 95 spills, up slightly from 93. EOG Resources ranked fourth with 46 spills, down from 59. Amplify nearly doubled its reported incident count year-over-year.

Source: WOGCC, 2025.
Volume

Top Operators by Volume Spilled, 2025 (Gallons)

Merit reported the highest volume at 303,565 gallons despite ranking seventh in spill count, reflecting a small number of large incidents. Continental ranked second at 177,000 gallons and Contango third at 134,000. Barn Owls Operating, which ranked sixth in spill count, reported nearly 64,000 gallons.

Combined oil, produced water, and other discharged volumes. Source: WOGCC, 2025.
Seasonality

Spills by Month, 2025

February was Wyoming's peak month with 119 reported spills. Winter months (January-March) accounted for a disproportionate share, likely reflecting freeze-thaw stress on pipelines and production equipment. Spill frequency dropped consistently through spring and summer before recovering slightly in December.

Source: WOGCC, 2025.
About This Report

Methodology

Colorado data is sourced from the Colorado Oil and Gas Conservation Commission's Spills database (Spills.zip, downloaded June 2025). Only spills categorized as "Recent" are included; "Historical" spills reported under Colorado's retroactive disclosure program are analyzed separately. Wyoming data was provided by the Wyoming Oil and Gas Conservation Commission upon request.

Volume figures represent the minimum amount reported by operators. Spills with unknown volumes are included in counts but excluded from volume calculations. New Mexico data was not available at time of publication.

Year-over-year comparisons for operators reflect company name standardization performed in the CWP Oil and Gas Spills Tracker notebook. Companies that merged or changed names may appear under their current or most common name.
